Micromobility

Background

1. In July 2020, the Department for Transport (DfT) launched a national rental e-
scooter trial. The trial period has subsequently been extended five times by the
DfT, with the current national trial extended to May 2028.

2. Rental micromobility services were introduced in the West of England in October
2020 as part of the region’s Future Transport Zone (FTZ) programme. The MCA
procured a concession contract for e-scooter rental services.

3. In January 2023, the MCA re-tendered the micromobility concession contract with
e-bike and e-cargo bike rental services added to the scope. This re-tendered
service was defined as the Integrated Micromobility Service (IMS). The MCA holds
the contract with the operator, and the MCA is responsible for the overall
management of the contract. The contract commenced on 7th July 2023.

4. The Unitary Authorities (UA) work collaboratively with the MCA and operator to
support the operational management of the service within their own authority area
and to support the sharing of best practice with other UA colleagues.

5. The number of daily trips now regularly exceeds 10,000. Highlights shown below
from the user survey undertaken by the MCA in summer 2025 demonstrate the
key role the micromobility service now plays in the regional transport system.

Key Considerations

6. The current IMS supplier contract expires on 6th July 2027 and cannot be
extended beyond the current 4-year contract duration. A new contract needs to
be procured to ensure continuity of the IMS in the region.

7. The new IMS Contract will be a concession contract procured under the
Procurement Act 2023.

8. The procurement documentation, including the contract specification and draft
commercial terms, are being drafted in parallel with the recommendations going
forward to MCA Committee. Engagement with Unitary Authority Directors on key
aspects of the tender is planned prior to the tender being released.

9. The IMS contract will include provisions that require the MCA to approve in
advance any changes to service parameters or operational requirements. This
may include, among other things: price increases, changes to discounts,
advertising permissions, approval of a penalty strategy, amendments to KPI
thresholds. These operational contract decisions are not explicitly covered within
the MCAs contract standing orders. A delegation is therefore requested to the
Executive Director of Transport to put operational contract decision-making
arrangements in place.

Alternative Options Considered

10. Not procuring a new IMS contract has been discounted because IMS is a key part
of the region’s transport system.

Consultation and Engagement

11. The IMS contract scope has been developed in close consultation between the
MCA and UA officers, and with feedback from Avon & Somerset Police and Avon
Fire & Rescue Service.

12. Feedback from users and non-users is regularly reviewed and will inform the
development of the specification of the IMS contract.

Key Risks

13. Limited market interest leading to a lack of competition in the procurement process
and reduced outcomes for the MCA.
• The West of England is an attractive market for on-street micromobility rental
companies due to the high existing usage of e-bikes and e-scooters. The risk
can therefore be sufficiently mitigated by developing a specification and
contract that responds positively to market feedback (where possible) and that
is in line with the best practice found across other UK and European markets.
• The project team has undertaken regular market engagement and research
during the existing contract. Pre-market engagement will be formally
undertaken to inform the contract specification and other procurement
documentation.

Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Implications

14. In progressing the proposed delegations relating to the micromobility tender, the
Combined Authority will continue to have due regard to its duties under the
Equality Act 2010 and the Public Sector Equality Duty. While this paper seeks
approval for governance and decision-making arrangements rather than the
contract specification itself, equality considerations will be embedded within the
subsequent development of the service model, procurement documentation and
contract management framework. This will include consideration of equitable
geographic access, affordability, safety, digital accessibility and engagement with
underrepresented communities, to ensure that the benefits of micromobility
contribute to inclusive growth and improved access to sustainable transport
across the region.

Climate Change Implications

15. Bristol City Council, Bath and North East Somerset Council, South
Gloucestershire Council and the Mayoral Combined Authority have all signed
climate emergency declarations. Following its declaration, the West of England
Climate and Ecological Strategy and Action Plan (CESAP) set out the
environmental priorities for the region. These priorities are reflected in the West
of England’s Growth Strategy, with the region aspiring to be nature positive and
have climate resilient and decarbonised transport infrastructure.

16. The IMS project is contributing to the improvement of public transport, which will
help to reduce car dependency and the associated greenhouse gas emissions,
air pollution and congestion. The project reports that carbon saving from users
switching from car/taxi/bus trips to e-scooters and e-bikes outweighs the carbon
increase from those switching from walking/cycling trips to e-scooter. The trial to
date has contributed to reduced carbon emissions across the region.

Financial Implications

17. There are no financial implications to the changing of the delegation regarding
procuring a new IMS contract. Once the procurement is underway, it is expected
that this will need to be led by the IMS project manager and this work will be
funded through current concession fees and the MCA share of any IMS revenue
share income. The new concession fees should ensure that once in place, running
the contract is cost neutral.

Legal Implications

18. Legal Services officers will work with IMS Service officers and Commercial officers
to support the procurement exercise for the new IMS contract. This will include
the selection and appointment of external legal advisers.

Human Resources Implications

19. There are no direct HR implications arising from this report. For situations where
there is a need for more staff resource than currently exists, HR will work with the
relevant officer/lead to ensure the request to recruit approvals and CLT approvals
are in place, followed by the Combined Authority’s recruitment or agency process
to appoint all posts – including fixed term, permanent, interim, agency or
contractor. Whilst not anticipated, in situations where funding is reduced, the
Combined Authority’s internal managing change and redeployment processes will
be applied where applicable

Commercial and Procurement

20. Pre-market engagement, and research regarding the IMS market and
financial/delivery models used are in progress. The findings of this will be used
to develop and inform the Procurement Strategy, tender documentation and
approach to market. The commercial team will develop the procurement strategy
based on the use of competitive flexible procedure and ensure that the MCA can
achieve best value for money.

21. As this is classified as a high priority gold contract (as prescribed by the MCA’s
contract segmentation tool), it is vital that the contract mechanisms allow for any
poor performance to be managed effectively and concession fee or equivalent is
bid at a realistic and achievable level.

22. It is anticipated that the commercial contract management activity will be managed
by the centralised Commercial Contract Management team.

23. Commercial will ensure appropriate resources are allocated to support and deliver
the procurement project. Commercial Board will be cited and approve the final
procurement strategy and contract award report once the tender process has
been completed.

Transport for City Regions (TCR) funding

Background

24. For the West of England Combined Authority area, the Transport for City Regions
(TCR) funding allocation is a total grant of £752m for the period 2027/28 to
2031/32, made up of £693m capital funding and £59m revenue funding. Revenue
funding has been allocated alongside TCR capital settlements to support the
development of investment pipelines, and to build longer-term local transport
planning and delivery capacity.

25. Since the confirmation of TCR funding in June 2025 the MCA has published its
Growth Strategy, which sets a clear direction for growth in our region including
North Somerset. The delivery of this important strategy is now a real focus and
funding sources, such as TCR, will need to be prioritised to ensure that growth
is realised. In addition, the West of England Transport Vision has been published,
which focuses on developing a transport network that:
• can be trusted
• is affordable, efficient to use
• offers more journey choice
• is ready for the future
• can enable our region to thrive

26. TCR and CRSTS funding now form part of a wider Local Transport Consolidated
Fund and DfT has commissioned Mayoral Strategic Authorities to produce a
Local Transport Delivery Plan (LTDP), setting out details of how all the available
funding has been prioritised, with details of schemes and interventions
or programmes and packages of spend. The MCA is required to submit a
detailed draft of the LTDP with all spending for 2026/27 and an outline plan to
2029/30 (2031/32 for TCR), by 20 March 2026 and a final LTDP to 2029/30
(2031/32 for TCR) should be submitted to DfT by 18 September 2026. DfT has
also set out its expectation that the LTDP is updated and published annually.

Key Considerations

27. As reported to the October 2025 Committee, this funding will be utilised to start
delivering against the Growth Strategy and continue delivering the region’s
adopted Joint Local Transport Plan.

28. The October 2025 Committee gave approval to committing £140.78m from the
TCR programme to fund the expanded CRSTS programme. This will
provide early delivery of projects within the TCR funding period. With other
commitments to revenue and maintenance there is £392.22m of TCR
funding remaining to commit.

29. As part of the programme development a number of place-based workshops were
held with unitary authority partners in December 2025. The purpose of these
workshops was to identify the transport interventions that were required to enable
growth in each of the growth zones. As part of this process a total of 91 scheme
proposals were identified. Of those schemes there are cost estimates for 52
schemes, which range from £888m - £1.7 bn.

30. In addition to the workshops with the UA partners input has also been sought from
the West of England Bus Operators Association because of the link to the update
to the Enhanced Partnership and the need to join up the planning of capital and
revenue investment.

Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Implications

47. The recommendations within this report have been considered in line with the
Equality Act 2010 and the Public Sector Equality Duty. Transport investment can
affect communities differently, particularly in relation to disability, age, sex and
socio-economic status. An inclusive-by-design approach will therefore be
embedded through delivery, ensuring that accessibility, safety, affordability and
equitable geographic distribution of benefits are considered as core principles.
Proportionate engagement and ongoing monitoring will support the identification
and mitigation of any unintended adverse impacts, aligning delivery with the
Combined Authority’s commitment to inclusive growth and improving quality of life
for all residents across the West of England.

Climate Change Implications

48. Bristol City Council, Bath and North East Somerset Council, South
Gloucestershire Council and the Mayoral Combined Authority have all signed
climate emergency declarations. Following its declaration, the West of England
Climate and Ecological Strategy and Action Plan (CESAP) set out the
environmental priorities for the region. These priorities are reflected in the West
of England’s Growth Strategy, with the region aspiring to be nature positive and
have climate resilient and decarbonised transport infrastructure.

49. Any modal shift from individual, combustion engine car use through electric bus
and active travel will see operational reductions in carbon emissions. Construction
of the supporting infrastructure also needs to factor in emission reductions, to
ensure the net impact is still an emission reduction. This means a strong focus on
adopting best practice in materials use and site management, as well as taking a
strategic view on decarbonisation of the network.

50. As the LTDP is developed in detail and moves to delivery, environmental
considerations should be integrated as early as possible. This includes ensuring
all infrastructure is resilient to both current and future climate impacts (based on
expected longevity of the project) and that all opportunities are taken to reduce
impacts on natural systems, and where feasible, enhancements made. Within the
overall funding envelope, a small but sufficient resource should be allocated to
ensure climate impacts are managed and nature positive decisions are fully
integrated.

Financial Implications

51. The TCR programme proposes a total expenditure of £939.78m, which is 25%
higher than the available budget of £752m. Given the historic trend of project
delivery taking longer than initially anticipated, this level of over-programming is
considered prudent to help maximise the use of available funding within the TCR
timeframe. As a result, it is expected that not all proposed projects will reach
completion within the £752m budget envelope.

52. The recommendation also seeks approval to allocate £10.5m of revenue funding
to a range of early development projects in 2026/27. Sufficient funding is available
to support this, with the MCA due to receive £9.51m in CRSTS and £2.766m in
TCR revenue funding during 2026/27. In addition, there is a forecast £16m
headroom carried forward from 2025/26. CRSTS and TCR programme support
activity is expected to utilise £11.5m of this funding, with any remaining balance
carried forward to support TCR development activity in 2027/28.

53. There is currently a TCR revenue funding deficit forecast in 27/28 of £5m,
meaning that not all of the development work set out can take place within these
two years. It is possible that the Mass Transit Programme will enter into the OBC
stage earlier and would therefore not need the full £12m revenue funding
suggested in the table above, as it would move to capital expenditure. Another
option that could be explored would be delaying some development expenditure
into future years so that the work schedule is more in line with the £11.34m we
will be receiving each financial year for TCR capacity funding.

74. The alternative options considered included not submitting a rebaseline change

DfT have been informed of the timescales and were aware that the programme
had previously planned to present a re-baseline report to Committee in October
2025 followed by detailed change requests from each authority. The grant
conditions for the programme require a change request, due to the change in the
grant conditions and the considerable number of changes in the milestones and
funding profile.

within.

CRSTS Board.

Key Risks

77. Contractually committed milestone – there is a risk that some schemes may
not be contractually committed by March 2027. The re-baseline change request
significantly reduces this risk with revised milestones confirmed by each
authority. However, approximately £72m of the programme’s allocation has
contractually committed dates close to the March 2027. In addition, some
concerns have been raised about the proximity of elections and the ability to
commit to construction projects when an administration might change. Officers
are reviewing options.

78. Cumulative impact of construction – as the programme delivery is back-ended
a programme wide risk has been identified regarding the cumulative impact of
construction on the transport network, communities and local economy in city
centres. The extension of the programme for an additional two years has
provided opportunities to re-phase schemes. In addition to this a review of the
programme has been carried out – including a cumulative impact assessment with
modelling. The results of which currently show that the impact is potentially less
severe than previously assumed, this is due to some re-phasing, increased
maturity of plans and improved data sharing. However, the data currently
includes a high degree of assumptions and unknowns. Therefore, this
risk remains a high rated risk for the
programme. Ongoing transparency, monitoring and collaboration between the
Combined Authority, Highways Authorities, transport operators and operations
teams is essential.

79. Impact on bus operations – there is a risk that due to the location construction
works, there will be significant re-routing of buses required, affecting the efficiency
and effectiveness of bus operations. The programme is working closely with
highways authorities and bus operators to manage impacts and consider bus
routes and bus stop locations. In addition to this the UAs and MCA teams run a
monthly session with bus operators to understand and support the impacts of
construction. Positive feedback on these meetings has been received to date.
80. Risk to benefits - Scope reductions may reduce benefits relative to cost, making
Value for Money (VfM) harder to demonstrate at FBC. Benefits and costs will need
to be assessed at FBC stage to ensure compliance with DfT requirements.

Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Implications

81. The recommendations within this report have been considered in line with the
Equality Act 2010 and the Public Sector Equality Duty. Transport interventions
can affect communities differently, particularly in relation to disability, age, sex and
socio-economic status. An inclusive-by-design approach will be embedded
through subsequent stages of development and delivery, ensuring that
accessibility, affordability, safety and equitable geographic distribution of benefits
are considered as core principles. Proportionate engagement and ongoing
monitoring will support the identification and mitigation of any unintended adverse
impacts, aligning the proposals with the Combined Authority’s commitment to
inclusive growth and improving quality of life for all residents across the West of
England.

Climate Change Implications

82. The MCA has committed to tackling the climate and nature emergencies, and
confirmed this with further statements in the regional growth strategy. Enabling
the delivery of transport interventions that provide alternative choices to using
internal combustion engine private cars can help to reduce their associated
environmental impacts. Projects should also ensure they are preparing for the
unavoidable impacts of climate change, ensuring resilience is built into every
stage. Projects that change scope should check that the benefits are still being
delivered.

Financial Implications

83. Any overspend within a delivery partner’s allocation will need to be dealt with by
cost efficiencies or scope adjustments of that programme; all the DfT funding has
been allocated and there is no contingency held in the central programme. The
programme still carries significant financial risks – immaturity of cost estimates
£295m pre-FBC, ability to forecast accurately, requirement to contractually
commit by March 27. Projects which have contractually committed milestones
close to March 27 have a value of £75.2m. Mitigation: Ongoing monitoring is
required on milestones, moving to cost of work done – project to be set up to
maintain focus and drive this work at more speed.

are in place, followed by the Combined Authority’s recruitment or agency process
to appoint all posts – including fixed term, permanent, interim, agency or

Combined Authority’s internal managing change and redeployment processes will
be applied where applicable

thresholds for approvals are in development and will further support programme
delivery.
