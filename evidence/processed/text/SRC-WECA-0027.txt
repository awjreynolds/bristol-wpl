Purpose of Report

This report represents the revenue and capital outturn for the West of England Mayoral
Combined Authority Committee for the financial year 2025/26 based on data from the period
April 2025 to March 2026 for both the West of England Mayoral Combined Authority and
Mayoral budgets.

Background
1. In accordance with the regular reporting timetable for updating the committee with budget
monitoring information, this report sets out the financial outturn position for both revenue
and capital budgets. Members of the committee are asked to note the outturn financial
positions set out in this report.
Revenue Outturn - West of England MCA

2. The 2025/26 West of England MCA Revenue Budget was originally set in January 2025
with income and expenditure of £173.9m, resulting in a balanced budget. This report
summarises the movement from the original budget through in-year forecasts to the final
outturn position.

3. Since the budget was set, the MCA has received new funding awards and reallocated
unused funds from previous years, increasing the current budget to £182.0m. As the year
progressed, delivery plans were refined and aligned to programme readiness, resulting
in a reduction in both expenditure and income to £174.0m at outturn. Further detail by
delivery theme is provided in Figure 2 and 3, with a breakdown of grant utilisation included
in Appendix 2.

4. The reduction in expenditure has increased the amount of cash available for investment,
boosting the treasury management surplus for 2025/26, as shown in Figure 6. This
surplus will be transferred to both the General Reserves and the Programme Risk
Reserve (Appendix 5) to strengthen the Authority’s financial resilience over the medium
term as we begin delivering major capital schemes and managing associated risks.

5. The table below summarises the revenue outturns against the approved budget (January
2025 committee) and the current budget. Movements reflect reprofiling of funding,
confirmation of new awards, and the firming up of estimates that were provisional at the
time of budget setting, now replaced with confirmed figures.

9. A summary of the underlying key variances by theme is set out in the following
paragraphs:

10. Economy and Skills Delivery: The £11.8m (23%) variance is largely attributable to Skills
Bootcamps (£7.0m). Smaller variances arise across the Mayoral Priority Scheme
(£1.0m), Business Innovation (£0.8m), Connect to Work (£0.7m), UKSPF (£0.6m), Adult
Skills Fund (£0.3m), Business Support Programme (£0.3m), Youth Guarantee (£0.3m)
and other smaller project variance coming to (£0.8m). The Skills Bootcamps variance
reflects the gap between the original budget assumption, based on 100% learner
completion and delivery of outcomes in line with Department for Education grant
conditions.

11. Environment Delivery: The £0.7m (10%) variance is primarily driven by the Green
Growth West Impact Fund of £0.4m, reflecting the early closure of the programme.
Unspent funding has been returned to the Investment Fund for reallocation. The
remainder relates to regional delivery on Low Carbon and Retrofit activity (£0.1m), with
other small variances across projects making up the remaining variance (£0.2m).

12. Transport Delivery: The variance of £8.47m (13%) against current budget is split
between Transport Operations of £5.6m, Transport Strategy of £0.7m & Transport
Delivery teams of £2.13m. Transport operations, covering buses, has seen a net
reduction over bus grant, BISP & the levy allowing £8.0m to be added into reserves,
helping to mitigate forecast cost pressures in 2026/27. Transport strategy has seen
underspend across the majority of its projects, which will be reprofiled into next financial
year. In Transport Delivery, spend on the West Rapid Transport project was £1.7m lower
than the budget, and this underspend has now been profiled into future years.

13. Homes & Regeneration Delivery: This area finished the year in line with budget, and
successfully secured additional funding during the year, including £0.5m from Homes
England in the final quarter. This funding supported the progression of the Hicks Gate
masterplanning work and the development of the Western Innovation Arc plan.

14. Strategy Delivery: The £0.5m (48%) variance is primarily driven by underspend within
the Digital Office project (£0.4m) due to reprofiling. The remaining variance is due to in
UK Mayors arising from staff vacancies through 2025/26, and the Regional Evidence
Portal Pilot arising in the current financial year pending completion of a change request.
The associated expenditure will be reprofiled into the next financial year.

15. Grant Management Delivery: The £1.2m (16%) variance reflects lower than budgeted
expenditure across several projects, primarily Somer Valley (£0.7m), Capricorn Quay
(£0.4m), and Western Harbour (£0.3m), partly offset by smaller movements elsewhere.
These variances relate to timing differences within agreed funding envelopes rather than
changes to the overall funding available.

30. Grant Funding: Grant funding remains the largest source of income at £97.2m, though
this is £16.5m (14%) below the current budget. The main movements include:
 Skills bootcamp reduced by £6.9m. The Skills Bootcamps budget assumed that
100% of learners would complete training and achieve outcomes in line with the
Department for Education (DfE) grant, whereas the outturn reflects lower actual
learner take-up and completion.
 Investment Fund income reduced by £6.7m as project delivery plans were revised
and change requests submitted.
 Adult Skills Fund income reduced by £1.4m following a reduction in grant from the
DfE. The shortfall has been funded from earmarked reserves.

32. Transport Levy: The Transport Levy includes an agreed £0.3m uplift for 2025/26 and a
further £0.4m underwrite provision, funded from external contributions, which was fully
utilised in-year.

33. The Levy continues to be subject to significant inflationary pressures and growth in
concessionary travel, particularly in relation to bus services. While resources have been
managed effectively to maintain service levels in 2025/26, these pressures are expected
to remain significant as services come up for renewal in 2026/27. Collaborative planning
with UAs will therefore be essential in developing a sustainable approach for future years,
review and engagement has already begun on this.

34. There was a £1.0m overspend in the Levy in 2025/26. The variance is primarily driven by
a £1.3m overspend in supported services, offset by variances across the other delivery
functions of Concessionary Fares (£0.2m), Community Transport (£0.3m), and Team and
Operational Costs (£0.2m). The other delivery functions have a combined variance of
£0.1m.

35. The increase in costs have been offset by £1.1m additional funding from S106, Clean Air
Zone (CAZ) funding, residual Local Transport Fund (LTF), and £0.1m income from non-
levy projects.

36. The net position of the Levy, resulted in a £0.6m transfer to reserves, which will be used
to alleviate any cost pressure moving into 2026/27.

37. Interest and Treasury Management: The Treasury return outturn is £22.3m, which is
£6.9m (45%) higher than budget. This reflects stronger cash balances available for
investment and higher interest rates than originally assumed.

38. Contributions, Fees and Charges: The outturn is £5.5m, which is £1.2m (26%) higher
than budget. This reflects updated delivery plans and revised assumptions on
contributions. Largest of these relates to supported buses (£0.5m), other smaller
variances largely offset each other.

39. Sales and Commission: Variance of £0.4m (8%) driven by renegotiation on the IMS
scooter contract to improve service and continuity for the area.

40. Business Rates: Business Rates income has increased by £0.5m (2%), reflecting
reconciliation adjustments following completion of prior-year NNDR3 and an additional
business rates relief payment. This additional income has been transferred to the
Business Rates Reserve to help mitigate future pressures associated with the anticipated
baseline reset in 2026/27.

There may still be further technical adjustments following submission of finalised NNDR3
returns by UA partners, with any subsequent movements expected to flow through
reserves.

50. Capital project expenditure is £203.7m for 2025/26, which is £35.2m (15%) lower than
the current budget of £238.9m.

51. Additionally, there was £0.4m spent on ICT and Office refurbishment during the year, a

Key Risks

62. This report forms a core part of the governance and risk management process. The
outturn presented in this report takes account of known financial risks and their potential
impact on the financial position for the financial year.

63. Treasury Management Income is currently being used to fund the increase in core staffing
costs, which is not a sustainable approach. As balances decline with ongoing delivery or
if interest rates fall, the available funding for core costs will decrease, requiring the
authority to either reduce staff or identify alternative funding sources.

64. The West of England MCA will continue to put forward a case to government to establish
sustainable core funding for Combined Authorities beyond the current annual allocations
(Mayoral Capacity Fund and Business Rates Pilot Scheme) and taking account of the
high inflationary environment that builds into base costs without inflated funding.
65. The organisation has been building a General Fund Reserve, in line with recommended
best practice, to manage / mitigate any future financial risks. However, the reserve
remains at a relatively low level being circa 6% of the 2025/26 revenue budget.

Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI) Implications

66. The proposals outlined in this paper present an opportunity to support inclusive growth
across the region. Consideration should be given to how different communities may
experience or benefit from these outcomes, particularly those who face existing barriers
related to access, socio-economic disadvantage or protected characteristics. Ensuring
that engagement, delivery and evaluation approaches are inclusive and informed by
diverse resident needs will strengthen both the impact and fairness of the work. Ongoing
monitoring of outcomes across different groups will also be important to identify and
address any unintended disparities.

Climate Change Implications

69. The MCA has committed to tackling the climate and ecological emergencies, and is intent
on embedding climate and nature into the growth strategy. Funding for climate and nature
outcomes will also be integrated into budgets across other delivery areas of the MCA,
especially transport and spatial planning. Where funds are allocated as grants to local
businesses and organisations, the criteria used to prioritise funding allocations will
incorporate carbon management and where appropriate, consideration of nature recovery
and climate resilience opportunities.

70. The MCA will continue to work with its Treasury Advisors, Arlingclose, for support and
advice through its Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) and responsible
investment service. As part of this process, we will ensure it aligns with our developing
Corporate Carbon Management approach.

72. General and earmarked reserves available to meet potential future risks remain

Human Resources Implications

75. The outturn position reflects improved workforce stability, with employee costs £0.5m
(2%) higher than budget due to lower vacancy rates than anticipated. This has
supported increased organisational capacity to deliver core activity and programme
priorities.

76. As the Authority transitions into a delivery-focused phase, workforce planning is shifting
from reliance on vacancy factors to a more strategic approach aligned to the Target
Operating Model (TOM), programme delivery requirements, and PMO-led oversight.
This includes ensuring clarity between core and programme-funded roles and
maintaining flexibility to respond to changing delivery timelines.

77. The continued roll forward of Organisational Improvement Plan activity into 2026/27
confirms the need for ongoing HR, organisational development, and change capability
to support structural design, consultation, and implementation.

78. A key risk remains the reliance on Treasury Management income to support core
staffing costs, which is not sustainable in the long term. This will require continued focus
on aligning workforce size and structure to a sustainable funding envelope, supported
by robust workforce planning and scenario modelling.

79. Equality, diversity and inclusion considerations remain important, with a focus on
ensuring that recruitment, organisational change, and workforce planning are delivered
in an inclusive and equitable way.

 Business Rates Reserve: Held to offset potential volatility in NNDR income from
changes to rateable values, exemptions, or collection rates.
 Integrated Transport Authority Reserve: Smooths variances in concessionary
fares and other transport costs between years, maintaining a revenue-neutral position
with Unitary Authorities.
 Integrated Transport Bus Reserve: A one-off £1.3m transport underspend from
2021/22 earmarked to protect bus routes, especially in rural areas.
 Bus Operations Reserve: Smooths variances in bus operation costs between years,
maintaining a revenue-neutral position.
 Mayoral and Housing Capacity Reserves: Support delivery of infrastructure and
strategic housing programmes by carrying forward unspent balances from national
funding allocation.
 Programme Delivery Overhead Recovery Reserve: Arises from central overhead
savings due to delays in programme delivery.
 Treasury Management Reserve: Mitigates financial risks from changes in interest
rates, cash balances, or pooled investment values. This includes the potential impact
of IFRS 9, should the statutory override expire in March 2029 — which could require
any future investment losses to be charged to revenue.
 UK Mayors Reserve: Ring-fenced for the M10 network of Mayoral Combined
Authorities, supporting joint working through a dedicated secretariat.
 Adult Education Reserve: Ring-fenced for future expenditure on adult education
services.
 Programme Risk Reserve: Provides critical cover against slippage, delay or
cancellation of capital schemes. With projected annual capital spend exceeding
£130.0m, maintaining a robust risk reserve is essential.
 Transformation Programme Reserve: Supports the ongoing organisational
transformation of the Authority.
 Integrated Mobility Fund (IMS): Accumulates revenue share from the Tier
micromobility contract. Funds will be drawn down once future use is formally agreed.
 Redundancy Reserve: Covers potential redundancy costs as short-term funding
streams end without confirmed replacement.
 4% Vacancy Factor Reserve: Reflects the Authority’s approach of budgeting staff
costs at 96% to account for natural turnover and recruitment lead-in times.
 Legal Reserve: Protects against unforeseen legal costs, safeguarding core budgets
and supporting robust governance.
 Organisational Improvement Plan Reserve: Established from underspend against
budget in year 1 to fund programme activity in year 2.
 ICT Costs Reserve: Established from underspends on budget in year to fund activity
that is planned in 2026/27.
 Other Reserves: Includes smaller earmarked balances such as the Brexit Fund,
Cultural programmes, Solar Together, and Rent Rebate reserves.
