Public Questions and Mayor’s Responses

West of England Combined Authority Committee and West of England Joint
Committee

Friday 27 March 2026

Question 1 – Mary Collett

Any potential reductions in emissions and improvements in air quality linked with a
mass transit scheme would be entirely wiped out if a mass transit scheme to Bristol
Airport was linked with approval for expanstion to 15mppa.

Can WECA assure residents that any potential contribution made by Bristol Airport
towards the cost of a possible mass transit scheme won't be linked to the potential use
of political influence to help make Bristol Airport's 15mppa planning application
happen.

Mayor’s Response:

Given the volume of questions received on this topic, it is unfortunately not
possible to respond to each question individually, but - for absolute clarity - mass
transit options and my position on any potential expansion of the airport are not
linked. It is also worth noting that today's meeting doesn't include any decisions on
either Bristol Airport or detailed mass transit proposals.

Any planning application in North Somerset will be decided by North Somerset
Council. I do not currently have a role in determining individual planning
applications, but believe that local people’s voices need to be heard in those
processes.

In July, I set out my position in relation to the airport, explaining that new laws
tabled in Parliament look set to provide regional mayors with new powers on
planning. For our part of the world, that would include North Somerset should they
join our combined authority. Mindful of that responsibility, my approach, now and
in the future, will be to carefully consider planning applications at the appropriate
stage, looking at all the evidence.

Last month, we published our Transport Vision and this sets out our ambitions for a
transport network that people can trust. We have a record transport funding
settlement of £752 million secured last year from government, and around £150
million is proposed to be allocated to developing mass transit. Council leaders and
I have a shared ambition to begin delivering the first mass transit projects within
four to five years. We see high-quality mass transit as being an essential ingredient
to connecting up our Growth Zones across the region, one of which is the North
Somerset Growth Gateway (which includes the airport).

We know that we need to make a compelling case to government for further
investment, and to do this we need to develop a business case for the scheme. The
first stage in this process was approved by this committee in 2024 and focused on
four key corridors, including one between Bristol city centre and the airport. This
corridor does not just serve the airport but could also provide sustainable
transport access to key development sites to unlock new housing and jobs, and
provide enhanced public transport access to parts of south Bristol that currently
have challenges with access to employment and services.

All of our local councils, including North Somerset, have been involved in work on
mass transit proposals to date and will continue to be involved as we bring the
project forward through the next stages of business case development. Public
consultation will also take place as the project develops.

Question 2 – CJ Lamb

Do you see it as the role of the mayoral combined authority under its strategic spatial
planning remit to facilitate the co-operative working, required by the 2011 Localism Act,
between the neighbouring local planning authorites over identifying transit or
'meanwhile sites' for homeless travellers so that Bristol, with shortage of land space
and a far bigger population than the rest, does not bear the brunt of providing sites for
the over 600 van dwellers within the city boundaries. Not only do neighbouring unitary
authorities have smaller populations, they also have larger land masses to
accommodate pitches.

Mayor’s Response:

The SDS is set to cover Bath & North East Somerset, Bristol, North Somerset, and
South Gloucestershire, as we work together to build the right homes in the right
places, with the services and infrastructure that communities need.

Emerging national planning policy (the Draft National Planning Policy Framework
2025), under which the Spatial Development Strategy (SDS) will be prepared, states
that a SDS should assess “the permanent and transit site accommodation needs
of travellers in the area.” As the Strategy develops, we are commissioning
evidence to further understand the breakdown of accommodation needs across
the region, and we’ll publish that in due course.

Question 3 – CJ Lamb

What are the plans during this parliament to approach the government for further
funding for the WE(MCA) transport 'vision', and is further funding feasible given the £45
billion government funding already committed to Northern Powerhouse Rail, £2 billion
for a new railway station in Bradford, £1 billion for a new tram link in Birmingham and
£14 billion for railways in Wales. Isn't the truth that WE(MCA) is well behind the loop in
terms of clout with government?

Mayor’s Response:

In this new chapter, we are banging the drum for our region. The West’s voice is
finally starting to be heard, as we work to catch up with other regions and make the
most of devolution.

Through the Transport for City Regions (TCR) programme, we have secured £752
million in further funding to invest by 2032. We have developed a Local Transport
Delivery Plan, which has been submitted to the Department for Transport (DfT),
which sets out a high-level allocation of £150 million for rail projects and the
significant development of our rail programme. It is our intention to use this
opportunity to develop our growing rail network and continue to make the case to
government for additional funding in future spending rounds.

We are having productive conversations with DfT and other departments, and our
Transport Vision last month was welcomed by the Secretary of State for Transport
as well as business and transport leaders across our region and beyond.

Question 4 – Councillor Rob Bryher

The Mayor recently attended the closing event of Playing Out at City Hall and expressed
disappointment that more School Streets are not being rolled out by Bristol City
Council. I share her frustration to some extent, but I would note that liveable
neighbourhoods schemes and modal filters more generally can deliver a lot of the
benefits of School Streets with the added benefit that they usually cover a much larger
area of a neighbourhood than just the 100 metres or so outside of a school. This has
huge potential benefit for children in terms of reducing road danger, air pollution and
other barriers to being able to walk, wheel, scoot and cycle to school.

Does the Mayor agree with me that we should rename low-traffic neighbourhoods and
liveable neighbourhoods as School Safe Travel Areas or Child Safe Travel Areas so that
it is clear to the public and politicians what the aim of these schemes really is?

Mayor’s Response:

I’ve long been supportive of School Streets, having started the programme in
Bristol while I was a councillor. We've secured more funding for active travel, and
our Transport Vision sets out an ambition to double the number of School Streets
around our region.

As you’ll know, we have commissioned an independent review of the East Bristol
Liveable Neighbourhood trial, considering all of the data. This is intended as a
comprehensive evaluation to inform future decision making, rather than a
rebranding exercise.

Question 5 – Councillor Rob Bryher
What is the current estimated cost to taxpayers of the independent review of the East
Bristol Liveable Neighbourhood trial scheme?

Mayor’s Response:

Once the independent review has been completed in the spring, we will share the
final costs when confirmed.

Question 14 – Charlie Siret

In her recent comments to the Bristol Post, the Mayor implied that franchising is too
expensive for the residents of the West of England. This assumes that a precept would
be a greater cost to the average resident than the current circumstances; however,
recent research from the University of Bristol found that "the silent cost of [Bristol’s]
selective transport system is already being paid by those least able to bear it." This
supports those we have spoken to in the West of England, who have told us that there is
a high financial cost of the unreliability and poor service provision of buses, as they are
missing out on job opportunities, healthcare, leisure, social support, and education, or
paying for petrol or using taxis to get there.

As the main reason given for rejecting franchising is a core assumption that a precept
for franchising would be more expensive to the average West of England resident than
improved bus services and infrastructure, can the Authority provide evidence to
support this claim?

Mayor’s Response:

We are committed to reforming our buses and improving services for passengers.

We are looking carefully at all of the options for how to take greater control of our
buses, with several different operating/funding models on the table. Franchising is
not a quick or cheap fix. It takes years and has meant an extra charge on people's
council tax bills elsewhere - which is not something we want to rush into. It's true
that what's right for somewhere else may not always be the best model for the
West of England, which is much more rural than other places, including Greater
Manchester, and so we're looking to make the right choice for us.

More immediately, we're already working to quickly improve our bus network, as
we secure more investment and make the most of devolution to catch up with
other regions. Over 250 new green buses will be on our streets by the end of this
year, with four electric depots, alongside offers like Kids Go Free over the school
holidays – including this Easter. In the coming weeks, we will be asking people to
have their say more generally on our buses to help shape our developing Bus Plan
as part of a transport network that people can trust.

Question 92 – Helen Hughes

WECA housing and transport policy is addressing a pressure on housing by the creation
of many new dense housing projects and 'new towns' Can you supply the modelling
(including the source) showing where this pressure is coming from, i.e. which
demographic groups are creating the pressure, the numbers and timescale, and why it
is occurring?
Mayor’s Response:

It’s important that housing is built in the right places with reliable transport
connections, access to nature, and where communities need it the most.

The housing crisis means 2,000 children in our region are living in temporary
accommodation, with Bristol experiencing the third-highest rate of any core city;
house prices in Bath have hit twenty-times average earnings; and 35,000 local
families waiting for social housing.

The government has set a target for the West of England to deliver 7,750 homes
each year, including in North Somerset. The government sets out housing targets
for each local authority using its ‘standard method’. This uses a formula that takes
0.8% of existing housing stock as a baseline, followed by an adjustment to increase
targets in areas with higher price-to-income affordability ratios.

Each local authority holds information at their local level within their emerging
Local Plans, and our Spatial Development Strategy (SDS) will look at this to better
understand our region’s housing needs. We are commissioning evidence for a
detailed breakdown of the data, and it will be published as this work develops.

Question 93 – Matt Sanders

QUESTION TITLE:

The Removal of Hyperlinks from Public Forum Statements

— INTRODUCTION

On Friday 20th March, I submitted a Statement for Monday’s Overview & Scrutiny
Committee.

This focused on a claim made on the Bristol City Council website, relating to the
South Bristol Liveable Neighbourhood. My statement therefore contained
a HYPERLINK, for readers to see the web-page in question, and understand the reason
for my statement.
My statement also included hyperlinks to maps and spreadsheets, published on the
BCC website, which I then used to prove that the SBLN statement was not true.

However, before publishing the submitted Statements, the Democratic Services
team removed all the hyperlinks from my text.

This made it difficult for committee members (and other readers) to follow the points
that I made, and to check the veracity of my conclusions.

I therefore queried this action, with the Democratic Services officer(s)…

— CORRESPONDENCE

On 20th March, the Officer wrote:

“Having been reviewed your statement will be published with the list of statements on
the Authority’s website prior to the meeting. However, we are unable to attach
hyperlinks, appendices or images to the statements we publish.”

I replied:

“… So why are you “unable” to include hyperlinks?

“Contributors to public forum of Bristol Council meetings routinely include links to
websites etc, and they are routinely retained in the published papers. Clearly that is
possible — the administrators are “able” to do it — as the simple act of copy-pasting
the text from the authors’ submissions will automatically bring the links with it. That is
easy. What is more work, is removing the hyperlinks.

“So you cannot possibly be “unable” to attach hyperlinks.

“Perhaps there is some rule forbidding links, for some reason…? But I cannot see any
such rule stated on the WECA website, or in the Constitution. If such a rule exists, then
can you please state where it is to be found, and send me the relevant document.

“… If you cannot quote any rule which forbids hyperlinks, then can you please retain the
links in my text. They are all to webpages on the Bristol Council website, which bring
clarify to the points that I am making, as well as allowing readers to check the veracity of
my statement.
“So there can be no good reason not to include those links.”

The Officer replied:

“We are reviewing the protocols around public engagement at meetings and will make
those available when the Constitution review due for ratification at June Committee is
made public.”

So, if those new proposals have not yet been ratified, then officers should still be
following the EXISTING rules — which DO NOT require the removal of hyperlinks.

The Officer continued:

“In the meantime, we follow an established practice which has been developed
following previous incidences of corrupted/broken links or those hi-jacked via malware
to other sites.”

But by simply clicking the links in my Statement, officers could easily verify that they
are NOT corrupted or broken, or hijacked via malware.

Just because that has happened on a few occasions in the past, it does NOT mean
that ALL links MUST be redacted in the future. That would be throwing the baby out
with the bath-water, for no good reason.

Democratic Services’ actions in this case should be proportionate to
the ACTUAL situation — and not an over-reaction to OTHER circumstances, which do
not apply to this case.

The Officer continued:

“We also do not endorse research or data from sources which have not been verified by
relevant officers in our own directorates.”

But the links are not to some obscure or dubious location. They are to the website of
Bristol City Council — WECA's largest partner.

And officers have not been asked to “endorse” anything.
Obviously WECA does not “endorse” the individual (often contradicting) Statements
submitted by multiple individuals. So nor would anybody see the hyperlinks in those
statements as being “endorsed” by WECA, or the Democratic Services team, or
anybody else. They have been included by the Statement’s author, to illustrate the
points they are making.

This is supposed to be free speech, so it would be actively dangerous, if links
were ONLY to be included if they were “endorsed” by officers.

Therefore, ALL links should be included in the published papers without question,
unless there is a legitimate cause for concern — eg being offensive or defamatory, etc…

Plus, of course, the fact remains that — as well as undermining my Statement for no
good reason — this action is patently UNCONSTITUTIONAL, as the new protocols
have not yet been ratified.
