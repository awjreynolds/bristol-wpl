Key Considerations

Distribution Hub

3. At the meeting of the MCA Committee in January, £50k of funding was approved
to develop the Full Business Case for the regional Distribution Hub which would
serve as a central site for redistributing surplus goods from business and public
sector partners to those who need them most, such as families on low incomes,
care leavers and other groups with specific needs. This will be delivered through
a partnership between the Combined Authority and Bristol Charities. At the
Committee £2m of capital was also approved in principle to acquire a site for the
Distribution Hub, subject to approval of the Full Business Case by the Committee.

4. The Multibank-type model is a clothes, bedding, baby, hygiene products, toys and
furniture bank all rolled into one, linking companies that have surplus goods
people need and local charities who know the people who need them. There are
currently 6 Multibanks in operation in the UK, but no Multibank style offer in the
South West. Multibank partners with companies such as Amazon, Unilever and
Ikea, among many others. Assessment of existing Multibanks or similar facilities
indicate that it would be reasonable to target a distribution of 800,000 essential
items to 19,000 families across the region from this element of the regional
distribution hub, once fully established.

5. Three quarters of Food Share South West’s (FSSW) charity partners serve
families with children and over 60% of what FSSW rescues is fresh fruit,
vegetables and dairy, alongside high-value proteins such as meat and fish. In
2024/5 they distributed 664 tonnes of food within the West of England Combined
Authority region, equivalent to £2.46m of food value, supporting 157 charities and
reaching approximately 17,700 people contributing 1.6 million meals.

6. Since January, significant progress has been made in developing the Full
Business Case, including work in progress to formalise the legal arrangements
between the Combined Authority and Bristol Charities. Bristol Charities has been
working with FareShare South West (FSSW) to secure a long-term solution to the
need for quality distribution space for FSSW and other regional charities with an
operating model requiring a warehouse or distribution/logistics space. The
partners are committed to scaling and widening the benefits of their work and
FSSW works not only across the Combined Authority region but also throughout
the wider South West. Initial economic modelling as part of the emerging Full
Business Case, shows the scheme has the potential to deliver strong value for
money. An Executive Summary of the proposal as it stands is set out in Appendix
2.

7. The forecast additional impact of FSSW in the West of England over ten years, in
the context of where the region’s most deprived areas are currently situated, is
shown in Figure 3. Please note this table shows FSSW’s current and future
projected support, not any other food distribution initiatives. The high level of need
in the Bristol area, combined with the maturity of FSSW's existing partner network
there, has meant that distribution is historically weighted towards this area.

Regional Distribution Hub project as an opportunity to analyse van routes and
other aspects of redistribution operations to assess how further support can be
provided into the other UAs within the West of England.

8. FSSW’s growth trajectory in Bristol started small so the current lower

Nature Recovery Fund

10. The Local Nature Recovery Strategy (LNRS) is the statutory document that lays
out the regional approach to nature recovery. The LNRS was developed through
a comprehensive engagement and consultation process, and represents a clear,
strategic approach to achieving nature recovery in the region. We have committed
to delivering the outcomes of the LNRS with a specific action in the Growth
Strategy and it will be a key requirement within the Environment and Climate
strand of the integrated settlement.

11. Delivery of the LNRS is being directly supported by the West of England Nature
Partnership (WENP). The partnership is hosted within the MCA, and includes all
4 UAs, representatives of the statutory environmental bodies, water companies
and the main environmental Non Governmental Organisations (NGOs). Following
publication of the LNRS, we have worked with WENP to develop more detailed
delivery proposals in the form of a prospectus, which is generating a pipeline of
specific projects.

12. It is proposed to allocate £5m of capital from the Investment Fund to create a
Nature Recovery Fund, to be deployed based upon the following approach:

• Take a steer directly from the LNRS.
• Be based on guaranteed delivery within the timeframes with projects being
close to shovel-readiness.
• Ideally involve multiple organisations to enable delivery of a wider a range
of outcomes.
• Be developed through convening partners to co-design and prioritise led by
WENP, supported by the MCA’s nature recovery team, to develop a small
number of strategic business cases.
13. The majority of funding is expected to support direct delivery and visible progress
within the short term, but a proportion could support the programme development,
evidence and investment-readiness work needed to leave the strategic pipeline in
a much stronger position for future investment and delivery.

14. It is planned that a number of Full Business Cases drawing on this allocation will
be presented to the Committee in October for approval, or in instances where
further time is required to develop the proposal, these will be submitted as Outline
Business Cases with a delegation sought from Committee for final approval.

Recommendation 2: Allocates £5m of Investment Fund capital to create a Nature
Recovery Fund.

West of England Cladding Remediation Funding Allocation

17. The Grenfell Tower tragedy, which led to the loss of 72 lives, highlighted
significant issues within the broader housing stock in England. Since then, many
thousands of buildings with unsafe cladding have been identified. Progress has
been made to tackle problems in those buildings most at risk, with remediation
having started or completed on 95% of high-rise buildings with the type of cladding
in place on Grenfell Tower.

18. In December 2024, the Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government
(MHCLG) published their National Remediation Acceleration Plan (NRAP) for
accelerating the remediation of high- and mid-rise buildings across the country.
The NRAP set out three key objectives:

• Fixing buildings faster.
• Identifying all buildings with unsafe cladding.
• Supporting residents.

19. The NRAP also set out new national targets for building remediation. It is expected
these will ultimately become statutory via the Remediation Bill. The targets were
updated in July 2025 to reflect that:

• By the end of 2029, all 18m+ (high-rise) buildings with unsafe cladding in a
government funded scheme will have been remediated.
• By the end of 2031, every 11-18m building with unsafe cladding will have
been remediated. Failure to meet this deadline without a reasonable excuse
will constitute a criminal offence, punishable by unlimited fines and/or
imprisonment.

20. To help accelerate remediation across the West of England, MHCLG has
indicatively allocated the Mayoral Combined Authority (MCA) £868,500 to
2028/29. MHCLG are not directly funding Avon Fire and Rescue Service (AFRS)
or the region’s Unitary Authority’s (UAs) with the exception of Bristol City Council
(BCC) and it is the expectation that the MCA deploy this funding appropriately.

21. Noting AFRS’s regional responsibilities and statutory remit, the MCA will seek to
passport the majority of funding to AFRS. In doing so, we can ensure, as much
as possible, a consistent and balanced regional approach to remediation activity.
It is on this basis that no allocations are proposed for Bath and North East
Somerset, North Somerset or South Gloucestershire Councils. Additionally, and
recognising the significantly higher number of mid and high-rise buildings in
Bristol, the remaining funding will be provided to Bristol City Council as a top up
to their allocation from MHCLG, with the MCA retaining a small fund to support
with coordination and analysis.

Zero Emission Bus Regional Areas scheme (ZEBRA 2)

25. In October 2023, the Department for Transport (DfT) announced the ZEBRA 2
fund – enabling local transport authorities to bid for funding to support the rollout
of zero emission buses across England. The West of England Mayoral Combined
Authority was successful with an initial bid of £6.6 million, to fund 74 new electric
buses and electrification of the Hengrove depot, which has now been fully
delivered. Funding was provided to First Bus through a grant funding agreement.

26. In December 2024, a bid for a scope increase was submitted to DfT and in March
2025, the DfT announced the award of an additional £19.8 million to fund 160
additional vehicles and charging infrastructure at Hengrove, Lawrence Hill and
Bath Depots. The total capital cost of the Authority’s ZEBRA 2 project now stands
at £134m (with some £26m from the ZEBRA 2 funding award and £108m of
investment from First Bus). The funding covers up to 75% of the cost of
infrastructure and 75% of the cost difference between a zero-emission bus and
conventional diesel buses and required partnership working with operators to
develop a suitable bid.

27. In July 2025, a delegation was granted by this Committee to Combined Authority
Executive Director of Transport, in consultation with the Unitary Authority Directors
of Infrastructure, to issue a grant variation letter in relation to the additional ZEBRA
2 award.

28. As the funding exceeds £25m a review by the Government’s Subsidy Advice Unit
(within the Competition and Markets Authority) is required to ensure that the grant
funding to First Bus is compliant with the Subsidy Control Principles. Only once a
referral has been made and completed can a grant agreement signed with the bus
operator. In November 2025 an application was made for an additional £11 million
in funding, but in March 2026 the MCA were advised that this bid for a second
scope increase was unsuccessful.

29. As a result of both the extended subsidy control process, and the need to wait for
the outcome of the additional funding bid, the MCA is no longer able to vary the
original grant funding agreement, as this agreement completed/expired in
February 2026, and a new grant funding agreement is required. On this basis,
new delegated authority is required to enter into a further grant funding agreement
with First Bus, related to the additional £19.8m award.

Zero Emission Transport City (ZETC) Funding Update

30. In January 2022, the DfT invited local authorities to submit Expressions of Interest
to become a designated Zero Emission Transport City (ZETC). Bristol City
Council, through the MCA as accountable body, submitted a successful bid and
secured £500,000 in development funding, which was confirmed in March
2022. In March 2023, the DfT revised its approach to the ZETC programme, and
subsequently paused the programme indefinitely. DfT has confirmed that the
funding may be repurposed to support business case development and policy
work.

31. In October 2025 the Committee noted that it was proposed to utilise the remaining
ZETC funding for a series of policy and feasibility studies that broadly align with
the original objectives of the ZETC initiative. Since October 2025 this work has
been further defined and relates to the development of the following policies and
plans:
• Transport Infrastructure Plan - setting out key infrastructure priorities for the
city for bus, walking, cycling and private vehicle networks. Developing a
scheme longlist that is costed and prioritised.
• Kerbside Strategy - setting out a strategy for how kerbside space can be
allocated to help the city achieve its aims around supporting active travel,
urban greening, electrification and accessibility among other priorities.
• Transport Investment Strategy - setting out a strategy for reinvesting
transport revenues generated from parking, bus gates, Clean Air Zone and
other sources to improve the transport offering.

32. The previous approval in October 2025 delegated authority to the Director of
Strategy and since this time the responsibility for this function has moved to the
Executive Director for Transport. In light of this change it is proposed that the
updated delegation supersedes the delegation approved in October and is to the
Executive Director of Transport.

Distribution Hub - Executive Summary

At this stage the proposal is still being developed and will be finalised once a suitable
site is identified and as such the details set out below are provisional.

Investment:
£4m blended investment (£2m MCA and £2m Bristol Charities) secures a long-term
regional asset.

The challenge facing the region:
• 67,000 children across the West of England live in poverty after housing
costs.
• Cost-of-living pressures are rising fastest in low-income households.
• At the same time:
o Tonnes of good-quality food and household goods are wasted.
o Voluntary, Community and Social Enterprise (VCSE) organisations are
being priced out of warehouse and logistics space.
o Demand for food, essentials and support continues to increase.
• Without intervention, charities will struggle to scale, and regional capacity to
tackle poverty and waste will shrink.

The proposal:
• The MCA will co-invest with Bristol Charities to acquire and repurpose a
regional distribution and logistics hub – delivering on goals in the region’s
Growth Strategy and Child Poverty Action Plan.
• The Hub will serve Bristol, Bath & North East Somerset, South
Gloucestershire and North Somerset (and beyond1).

1 FareShare South West works right across the South West down to Cornwall, and a larger central

distribution hub will enable scale-up across the whole area
• It will provide:
o Affordable, fit-for-purpose space for surplus redistribution.
o Shared infrastructure for regional VCSE partners.
o A platform for employability, skills and inclusion.
• This is a MCA-wide asset, designed to reach urban, suburban and rural
communities.

What the Hub will deliver:
Food redistribution at scale
• Enables FareShare South West to increase food volumes by 23.5%.
• Delivers 4.3m additional meals across the region.
• Supports over 170 frontline charities serving families with children.

A first for the South West
• Establishes the first Multibank-type service in the region.
• Redistributes surplus including baby items and bedding, school and
household essentials, hygiene and period products.
• Directly reduces the cost of living for families.

Contribution to the region’s Growth Strategy
• To support delivery of the following outcomes in the West of England’s
Growth Strategy Outcomes Framework:
- 6.1.1 increasing engagement with support, advice and information for
families.
- 6.1.2. Increase engagement in activities and education for children and
families.
o 6.1.2.5 Value of cost savings for families.
- 6.3.2 improving quality of life in areas of deprivation.
- 6.4.1 improving the wider determinants of health.
- 6.4.2 Improve child health: emotional and physical wellbeing.
- 4.2.3 reducing geographical employment rate gaps for residents of areas of
deprivation.
- 5.2.1 Reduction in direct carbon emissions from industry and commercial
operations, including from buildings upgraded.

Economic case
• Initial modelling work shows strong value for money2:
o Benefits include reduced poverty, reduced public spending, avoided
disposal costs, improved health outcomes, increased employment.
• Bristol Charities receives rental income (c.6% yield) reinvested into charitable
activity; WECA receives long-term capital appreciation.
Skills, jobs and inclusion
• The Hub will act as a regional employability asset, creating 120 structured
pathways including work experience, training and qualifications, and

those with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND).
Environmental impact
• Tackles poverty and pollution together by preventing 3,600+ tonnes of CO₂e
emissions from food waste alone, plus further prevention through reuse of
household goods.
• Supports a circular economy across the West of England and embeds MCA
climate and sustainability priorities.
In summary:
• A single regional asset, delivering multiple MCA priorities.
• Measurable impact on child poverty, cost of living, skills and climate.
• Clear benefits across all four constituent authorities.
• Strong value for money through co-investment and shared infrastructure.
