# 3. The essential conceptual distinction

This programme has **two connected but different tracks**.

## 3.1 Five Case Model business-case track

The OBC and FBC provide structured decision support through five dimensions:

1. **Strategic Case** — the case for change, objectives, policy fit, scope, dependencies and expected outcomes.
2. **Economic Case** — options appraisal, social costs and benefits, distributional and place-based effects, uncertainty and value for money.
3. **Commercial Case** — the operating model, procurement, market capacity, contracts, risk allocation and service outputs.
4. **Financial Case** — implementation cost, operating cost, charge income, net proceeds, affordability, accounting, borrowing and cash flow.
5. **Management Case** — governance, delivery, consultation, risk, assurance, benefits, evaluation, transition and operational readiness.

The OBC should normally validate the case for change, appraise a credible shortlist and identify a preferred option sufficiently developed to consult on and progress.

The FBC must update all five cases using final scheme design, consultation evidence, final cost and revenue assumptions, commercial arrangements, delivery plans and statutory requirements. It must demonstrate readiness for the actual approval sought.

The programme must create a SOC-equivalent strategic assessment before OBC detailed appraisal. This may be called the Strategic Assessment / Preferred Way Forward Report if no formal Strategic Outline Case is commissioned. It must contain the case for change, Business as Usual/Do Minimum, SMART objectives, critical success factors, longlist, options framework filter, rejected options, shortlist, preferred way forward, and evidence that the preferred way forward has not predetermined the OBC preferred option.

## 3.2 WPL statutory licensing-scheme track

A WPL is not only a transport investment proposition. It is a statutory licensing scheme that may impose charges in respect of workplace parking places at premises within a defined area. The statutory track must address, at minimum:

- the identity and legal powers of the licensing authority or authorities;
- the scheme area and legal boundary;
- the definition and treatment of premises, occupiers, associated persons and workplace parking places;
- charge liability, charge classes, thresholds, exemptions, discounts and indexation;
- licence application, variation, surrender and record-keeping;
- inspections, compliance, penalty charges, representations, enforcement and appeals;
- commencement, transition and any shadow operation;
- the use and accounting of gross revenue and net proceeds;
- the linkage of net proceeds to local transport policies;
- the required general and detailed plans for the application of net proceeds;
- consultation and response to material representations;
- local decision-making and order-making;
- confirmation by the Secretary of State or any applicable exemption;
- monitoring, review and amendment.

A Five Case Model document is **not automatically the statutory scheme order or the complete confirmation submission**. Conversely, a legally adequate order does not by itself demonstrate value for money, affordability or deliverability.

Before using the phrase “statutory confirmation dossier” as a settled route, produce a statutory route note distinguishing: initial scheme order, variation, revocation, RPI-only variation, and any other amendment. For an initial scheme, treat Secretary of State or appropriate national authority confirmation under Transport Act 2000 section 184 as required unless current law and legal review establish otherwise. Do not transpose a variation exemption to an initial order.

Maintain a reconciliation table at:

`statutory_dossier/business_case_to_statutory_crosswalk.csv`

It must map every statutory requirement to the relevant OBC/FBC section, statutory document, evidence item, owner, reviewer and status.

# 8. Multi-agent organisation

Explicitly spawn specialised subagents. Codex only uses subagents when asked; this instruction asks for them.

Use separate worktrees or clearly separated branches where supported. Each subagent must have a bounded scope, a file ownership boundary, a required return format and a reviewer. Do not allow two agents to edit the same main file concurrently.

Context management is a quality control. Do not overload subagents with the full programme unless their role genuinely requires it. For each subagent, create a bounded task packet containing:

- role and simulated competence;
- exact question to answer;
- files and source IDs to inspect;
- files the agent may edit, if any;
- files the agent must not edit;
- evidence hierarchy and acceptance criteria;
- maximum output length;

Use that packet to select the mandatory first-read files, domain add-ons, banned claims and escalation path before opening long reports, full source packs or raw evidence. Subagents should receive bounded task packets rather than the whole context set.

Subagents must not infer from memory when a cited source is available. If a source is unavailable, they must say so and record the gap. A subagent may sign off only the scope in its task packet. The Programme Orchestrator must synthesize subagent outputs and check for contradictions before any stage-gate recommendation.

## 8.0 Stage continuation and context-bloat control

Future stages must be run as bounded stage packages, not as broad repo-reading exercises. Before substantive drafting or review, the main agent must read `instructions/20-stage-continuation-and-context-control.md` and apply it.

For each new stage, the main agent must:

1. start from the latest README, stage index, stage-gate plan, previous gate report and the most relevant domain context packet;
2. create or update a compact stage context packet listing scope, out-of-scope files, source IDs, open issue/risk/gap IDs, no-go claims, validators and required register updates;
3. spawn specialised subagents with bounded task packets and `fork_context=false` by default where tooling supports it;
4. use at least domain, evidence/citation and red-team or public/officer readability review lanes for substantive stages;
5. prohibit subagents from reviewing "the whole repo" or inferring from memory where a cited source exists;
6. synthesize subagent outputs, resolve contradictions and update the stage artefacts and registers;
7. run focused validators, `make validate`, `git diff --check` and public-repo secret checks before claiming completion;
8. record focused and full command evidence in `evidence/validation/validation-run-register.csv` and a stage-specific validation log where the stage materially changes public/officer navigation, validators, registers or gate reports;
9. commit and push the completed stage before moving to the next stage, unless the user has prohibited pushing or the remote is unavailable.

Validation evidence is process evidence only. It can record command text, run date, repo state, exit code and output summary. It does not prove evidence truth, source currentness, legal correctness, substantive gate judgement, professional assurance, blocker completeness, risk adequacy, mitigation adequacy or WPL readiness.

Subagent context packets must name exact files and source IDs to inspect, allowed write paths if any, maximum output length, review criteria and no-go claims. # 21. Red-team mandate

The Red Team must presume that the draft may be wrong and test it.

At minimum challenge:

- Is the case for change supported by data?
- Does WPL materially address the identified traffic, or only a subset of commuting traffic?
- Is the intervention primarily demand management, revenue raising or both?
- Are objectives internally contradictory?
- Is the Do Minimum realistic?
- Were non-WPL alternatives treated fairly?
- Is the preferred boundary selected for evidence or convenience?
- Is the liable-space base overstated?
- Is hybrid working adequately treated?
- Is employer pass-through assumed without evidence?
- Are business relocation and employment effects understated?
- Is the regional labour market properly represented?
- Are cross-boundary and residential displacement effects credible?
- Are public transport alternatives available before charging?
- Can buses/trains absorb mode shift?
- Are WPL and funded-project benefits double counted?
- Is gross revenue misrepresented as social benefit?
- Does revenue decline if the policy succeeds?
- Are exemptions simultaneously claimed as equitable and ignored in revenue/effectiveness?
- Are public-sector charges simply moving money within government while creating budget pressure?
- Is the investment programme mature enough to spend proceeds lawfully and effectively?
- Are fixed commitments being made against volatile income?
- Is WECA/MCA support overstated?
- Is DfT confirmation treated as automatic?
- Is consultation genuinely formative?
- Have material adverse responses been addressed?
- Are equality mitigations deliverable and funded?
- Is the enforcement service realistic?
- Is the data lawful, sufficient and maintainable?
- Are technical uncertainties visible to members?
- Would a reasonable decision-maker understand why the recommendation follows?
- Could the case withstand judicial review, audit and public scrutiny?
- What evidence would reverse the recommendation?

Classify findings:

- `P0 Critical` — invalidates legality, decision integrity, affordability or core evidence; stage must stop.
- `P1 Major` — material deficiency requiring closure or approved condition.
- `P2 Moderate` — improvement needed but not necessarily gate-stopping.
- `P3 Minor` — editorial or low-impact issue.

P0 always blocks the relevant simulation gate. P1 blocks the gate unless a named simulation gate authority accepts a written condition, owner, deadline and residual risk. P2 does not block unless repeated, systemic or affecting a decision-critical claim. P3 cannot change a recommendation.

Record every finding, response, owner, due date, evidence and closure reviewer.
