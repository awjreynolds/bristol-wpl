# Stage 1 Comparator Evidence Review

Status: simulated due-diligence review. This is not transport-economics, legal, financial or professional sign-off.

## Decision

Simulation sign-off with conditions.

The acquired comparator evidence is useful for cautious operating precedent, transferability questions and lessons from other places. It does not support direct Bristol impact, revenue, congestion, employment or mode-share claims.

## Evidence Inspected

- `SRC-NOTT-0001`
- `SRC-NOTT-0002`
- `SRC-ACADEMIC-0002`
- `SRC-TFL-0001`
- `SRC-UK-0003`
- Acquisition and extraction logs for `SRC-ACADEMIC-0001`

## Findings

- `SRC-ACADEMIC-0002` is the strongest acquired comparator for behavioural response, but it does not directly prove cause and effect.
- `SRC-ACADEMIC-0001`, the intended independent congestion study source, failed acquisition and is not usable.
- Nottingham official material can inform scheme narrative, licensing/compliance machinery, employer support and reported reinvestment categories, but cannot transfer Bristol revenue or impact estimates.
- TfL material is useful for process controls and displacement/consultation issues, but its legal and approval machinery is London-specific and dated.
- Leicester material is useful for proposal-stage/non-implementation lessons, not as post-implementation impact evidence.

## Conditions

- Acquire a usable independent congestion evaluation source or omit independent congestion-reduction claims.
- Refresh current Nottingham charge, threshold and latest reporting evidence before using current operational comparisons.
- Build a Bristol-versus-Nottingham transferability matrix covering boundary, parking inventory, public transport alternatives, employer mix, charge pass-through, displacement, governance and funded programme.
- Use cautious wording such as "Nottingham reports", "survey evidence suggests" and "potential comparator lesson".

## Simulation Caveat

This is simulation-only and has no real-world professional, statutory, legal, financial or officer sign-off effect.
